Seasonal Tips (May–June)

  • Late May / early June begins tsuyu (rainy season). Carry a compact umbrella daily from around May 28 onward.
  • Temperatures: 18–26°C. Light layers, breathable clothes, comfortable walking shoes.
  • Expect 12,000–18,000 steps on active days. Feet first — fashion second.
  • Mt. Fuji visibility in May is 40–60% — go for the experience regardless.

Money & Practicalities

  • Japan is still heavily cash-based. Carry ¥10,000–25,000 per person on active days.
  • 7-Eleven, Family Mart, and Lawson ATMs accept international cards reliably.
  • Pick up pocket WiFi or a SIM card at Narita upon arrival (desk in arrivals hall).
  • No tipping — ever. It can be considered rude.
  • Google Maps is excellent for Japanese train navigation.

Good to Know — Practical Tips

  • Shinkansen oversized luggage: Suitcases with total dimensions 160–250cm must be placed in a reserved "oversized luggage space" behind the last row of seats — and you must reserve that specific seat in advance. Most standard luggage (carry-on + medium suitcase) falls under 160cm and is exempt. Check your bag dimensions before travel.
  • No public rubbish bins: Japan has almost no street bins outside convenience stores. Carry a small plastic bag for your trash at all times. Convenience stores (7-Eleven, Family Mart, Lawson) have bins — use them. You'll get used to it quickly.
  • Suica card loading: Load ¥3,000–5,000 at airport machines on arrival. Top up at any konbini, JR machine, or subway station. The card works on nearly every train, metro, bus, and as payment at most convenience stores.
  • Google Translate camera: Point your phone camera at any Japanese menu and Translate will overlay the English translation in real time. Indispensable for hole-in-the-wall restaurants with no English menu.
  • Restaurant plastic models / ticket machines: Most specialist restaurants (ramen, gyukatsu, etc.) use vending machines at the entrance — insert money, select your dish, get a ticket, hand it to the staff. English options are increasingly common. For restaurants with no ticket machine, plastic food models in the window show the dishes — point at what you want.
  • Shoes on / off etiquette: Remove shoes at traditional restaurants with tatami seating, temple interiors, and many ryokan. Look for a raised entry area (genkan) and a collection of slippers — that's your signal.

Gyukatsu Motomura Dinner — Tokyo's Most Famous Beef Cutlet

Gyukatsu Motomura — Shinjuku South Exit Branch (牛かつもと村 新宿南口店) — B1F, 3-32-2 Shinjuku, Shinjuku-ku, Tokyo 160-0022. Open daily 11am–11pm. A 3-minute walk from Shinjuku Station South Exit. This is the chain that invented Tokyo's gyukatsu obsession — and is now consistently ranked one of the most essential restaurant experiences in the city.

What is it: deep-fried beef cutlet (rare-to-medium, breaded in panko) served with a small personal hot stone to finish grilling each piece to your exact doneness preference. The set (¥1,930–3,060 depending on size) comes with barley rice (free refills!), miso soup, cabbage, potato salad, pickled veg, and warabi mochi dessert. Four dipping sauces: wasabi soy, horseradish, rock salt, and shoyu. The 1.5 cutlet set (¥2,600) is the sweet spot for most appetites.

Queue strategy: Waits can hit 45–90 min at peak times (noon–2pm, 6–8pm). Go at 5:00–5:30pm on a weekday before the dinner rush — many report walking straight in or 15-minute waits at that window. Alternatively, reservations are now available via tablecheck.com — book in advance.

Post-Dinner: Omoide Yokocho + Golden Gai
  • Omoide Yokocho (Memory Lane) — west side of Shinjuku Station. Look for it on Google Maps. A smoke-filled alley of 20+ tiny yakitori stalls, unchanged since the 1950s. Even just passing through after gyukatsu for a beer and skewer is the move. Best 6pm–midnight.
  • Golden Gai — 5-minute walk from Omoide Yokocho, Kabukicho area. A labyrinth of 200 tiny themed bars, each seating 5–8 people. Look for English-welcoming signs. One drink per bar, then wander to the next. Avoid any bar where someone tries to recruit you from the street.
The Full Shinjuku Eating Plan

Lunch: Udon Shin (if queue works out) — handmade noodles in a 12-seat alley shop, Tabelog Top 100. Dinner: Gyukatsu Motomura Shinjuku South — interactive tableside rare beef cutlet, ¥2,600/person for the 1.5 set. After: One drink at Omoide Yokocho, one bar at Golden Gai. This is a flagship Shinjuku night.

Nagoya Food Signatures

Added: Himeji Castle — Morning (Perfect Use of This Flex Day)
  • This flex day is the ideal moment for Himeji — Japan's most beautiful castle, universally rated as the finest surviving feudal castle in the country. The morning works perfectly: Shinkansen there and back, back in Osaka by early afternoon for Harukas.

Himeji Castle gets very crowded by 10am. Arriving at opening (9am) after a ~8am Shinkansen departure gives you 45–60 min of relatively quiet castle access before the tour groups arrive.
Himeji Specialty

Himeji oden — unlike any oden in Japan. The dashi broth is infused with ginger soy sauce and served with a brush of fresh ginger paste on the side. Vendors cluster near the castle entrance. Eat it standing. It costs ¥200–400 per piece and is one of those quiet regional experiences that stays in memory long after the bigger attractions.

Yanaka Old Town · Ueno · Fukagawa Hidden Gem

  • Good low-stress day after the Osaka transfer. From Shiomi hotel: JR Keihin-Tohoku Line to Tokyo Station (~15 min), then JR Yamanote Line to Ueno (~5 min). Very straightforward.
  • Yanaka district (morning) — a 10-minute walk northwest from Nippori Station (one stop from Ueno on JR Yamanote). One of Tokyo's most atmospheric and least-touristed old-city neighbourhoods. Survived WWII bombing and the postwar reconstruction wave intact — the wooden temples, craft shops, and family businesses remain essentially unchanged from the Showa era.
  • Walk Yanaka Ginza covered street — independent butchers, tofu makers, senbei shops, and eccentric cat-themed everything. Deeply local. Pick up menchi katsu (minced meat croquettes, ¥150) hot from a butcher's stall.
  • Yanaka Cemetery (optional) — an unusually beautiful urban cemetery with hundreds of old cherry trees and moss-covered graves, including the final resting place of the last Tokugawa shogun. Serene and oddly moving.
  • Ueno Park (afternoon) — a slow stroll through the pond, past the zoo, and up to Tokyo National Museum (9-35 Ueno Park, Taito-ku; open Tue–Sun 9:30am–5pm; adults ¥1,000) if anyone wants Japan's premier art collection. Alternatively, the Shinobazu Pond rowboats are relaxing (¥700/30 min).
  • Ameyoko Market — the bazaar between Ueno and Okachimachi stations under the JR tracks. Dried seafood, cheap cosmetics, clothing, and street snacks. Good for a late afternoon graze.
Hidden Gem Dinner: Fukagawa Meshi — Tokyo's Forgotten Soul Food

Since you're based at the Shiomi Prince Hotel (Koto Ward), you're very close to Fukagawa — one of Tokyo's oldest fishing districts. Try fukagawa meshi for dinner: a dish of clams and long onion slow-cooked in miso, served over rice. It was the humble everyday food of the Edo-era fishermen who lived here, and it's nearly impossible to find outside this neighbourhood.

Miyako Restaurant (2-7-1 Tokiwa, Koto-ku, Tokyo 135-0006; open Tue–Sun 11:30am–2pm, 5:30–8:30pm; closed Mon) — a quiet traditional spot with tatami seating. No English menu; just say "fukagawa meshi set" — they understand. Simple, local, and one of those genuinely off-beaten-path Tokyo experiences. The walk from your hotel is about 15 minutes through the old downtown.

Today's Flavours

Yanaka: Menchi katsu from a butcher stall, fresh tofu from a neighbourhood maker, senbei (rice crackers) bought warm from a small shop. All under ¥500. Ameyoko afternoon: Graze the food vendors — yakitori skewers and fresh fruit cups. Dinner: Fukagawa meshi at Miyako Restaurant — comfort food from Edo-era Tokyo, right on your doorstep.
